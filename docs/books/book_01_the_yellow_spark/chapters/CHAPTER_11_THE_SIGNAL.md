# CHAPTER 11 — THE SIGNAL

The single shoot of grass did not stay single.

It took a few days, but the green that Zaro and ARC-7 had made together did not all gutter out when they collapsed. A little of it had soaked into the dead ground where it bloomed, and that little did what living things do. By the third morning the one shoot was a patch. By the fifth it had reached the foot of the scarred tree, a thin green tide creeping up against the black, and where it touched the roots, the lowest branch put out three new leaves, small and tentative and the deep green that did not stop at the surface.

The tree was not healed. Most of it was still black, still scarred, the gold mark still lit in the trunk where Zaro had carved it. But it was not all dead anymore, and Zaro visited the three new leaves every morning the way he had once visited the gold line, and told them they were doing a good job, because they were.

He was coming back too, faster now, as if the green had given him something. He could hold the lamp through a whole night again. He could make a small dome, briefly, if he had to. He was not what he had been, and he understood now that he might never be exactly that again, but he was enough to stand and to fight, and for now that was enough to be going on with.

Then, on the sixth day, the sky tore again, and it was not ARC-7's door.

The Fireflies, the two that were left and the four Mina had rebuilt, all screamed at once.

It came across the wilted garden, and it was not the dark the way the flood had been the dark. The flood had been formless, a tide with no center. This had a shape. It walked. It was tall, taller than ARC-7, cased head to foot in something that was not metal and not stone but the Ink itself gone hard and bright-edged, a figure of black that drank the dawn light and gave nothing back, and it carried the cold with it like a weather.

ARC-7 went very still, which Zaro had learned was its version of alarm. And then it went stiller than that, in a way Zaro had not seen, and when it spoke the level voice could not quite hold flat.

"That is not a probe," it said. "That is not the hand. That is one of mine."

"One of yours," Mina repeated.

"When an Observer goes silent, the others send someone to recover him. I went silent the night I crossed." The cyan light flickered, the way a steady hand shakes only when its owner has understood, too late, what they have done. "They sent that one across the dark to find me. And with no world around him to shield him, the dark found him first. It caught him on the road in, and it armored him, and it made my own rescue into the thing it sent to end me." A pause. "There is someone in there. One of my own kind. The cost of my crossing, wearing a coat of the dark."

The armored thing reached the edge of ZaroLand and raised one black hand, and the new grass under it died in a spreading circle, fast, the way the deer's clover had died, and Zaro felt the old grief and a new fury come up in him together.

They fought it, at first, the way they had fought the flood, and it did not work, because this was not a flood. Mina called it and ARC-7 caught it and Kai marked it, and it walked through the cyan lines as if they were not there, because it was not formless dark that could be pinned. It was a thing with a will, and the will was strong, because it had belonged to a hero.

But Zaro, staying lit at the center, watching it come, saw the thing the others could not see from where they stood.

The eyes.

Inside the black helm, where there should have been nothing, where the dark should have been only dark, there were two eyes. And they were not the dark's eyes, flat and hungry. They were a person's eyes. And they were screaming.

"Stop," Zaro said. "Stop, everybody stop. There's someone in there."

"Zaro, it's killing the grass."

"There's someone in there!" He was moving now, toward it, the way he had moved toward the moth, toward the cold stone, toward every hurt thing he had ever refused to leave. "It's not the dark. It's someone the dark is wearing. Look at its eyes. Look."

He did not know if the Seal could do this. He only knew what it had done to the small dark, filled it, crowded it out, and he knew what the dark had done to this person, hollowed them and worn them, and he thought, with no proof and all his heart, that a thing which could fill an absence might be able to fill a person back up.

"ARC-7," he said. "With me. Like before. But not to clear it. To free him."

ARC-7 looked at the screaming eyes in the black helm, and made a calculation, and for once did not say the odds aloud.

"That is not what the Seal is for," it said.

"It's what I'm for," Zaro said.

A beat. Then ARC-7 came to him and reached, the way it had learned to reach, and the cold cyan came up against the warm gold. This time Zaro was not nearly empty. This time he had six days of sun and three new leaves' worth of hope in him, and the green that bloomed between them was deeper and brighter and steadier than the desperate flicker of the battle.

And the others did the thing they had learned they could do. Mina, who could see where everything was, saw where the person was inside the armor, the small trapped human-shaped warmth buried under all that hard bright black, and she called it. "Lower. Center. There's a knot of warm in there, that's him, that's the person, put it there." And Kai, gloveless still but stronger bare-handed every day, took the green light the way he took the dust and pushed it, lent it the shape of his want, aimed it where Mina said.

The Verdant Seal hit the armored thing and did not push it back.

It went in.

The green poured into the black, into the hollowed place where someone had been, and it filled what the dark had emptied, and the bright-edged armor cracked, and cracked again, the Ink boiling off it like dark water off a thing lifted into the sun. And as it burned away, the green found something underneath that was not the Ink. A pattern of light on the figure's chest, an insignia stuttering one command over and over, a loop that had run so long it had worn a groove in him. The green filled that too. The loop broke, and went quiet, and under it, slowly, a shape that had been hollow became someone again.

The armor fell away in flakes of dying black, and a figure sank to its knees in the new grass. Not a man. He was built on the same cold precise lines as ARC-7, the same made angles, the same clean light, except his ran the pale gold-white of a thing that had been a long time in the dark and was only now remembering it could shine at all.

He looked at his own hands, unarmored, his. Then he looked at ARC-7, and something passed between the two made beings that Zaro had no word for.

"ARC-7," the freed one said, in a voice like a door opening in a house no one had lived in for years. "You went silent. They sent me to bring you back." He looked down at the last of the dark sloughing off him. "I did not arrive the way I was sent."

"No," ARC-7 said. "You did not."

The freed one turned his regard on the four of them, on the squirrel in the tree, on the small green tide, on the scarred tree with the gold mark. "You did not destroy it," he said. "You could have. The clean solution was to end the carrier. You filled it instead. You saved the one it was riding."

"You're not an it," Zaro said. "You're a someone."

The freed one looked at him for a long moment, the way ARC-7 had looked at him on the very first day, a made thing measuring a thing it had no column for.

When he could speak again, he told them what little the dark had left him able to say. It had held him a long time, and used his own mission as a leash. But he had felt, through all of it, what the dark felt, and tonight what it felt was new.

"It felt that," he said, looking at the place where the Seal had bloomed. "The filling. Worlds do not fight back. Worlds go out. Yours did not." He looked at Zaro with something between warning and wonder. "An absence that has eaten a thousand worlds and never once been refused has, tonight, been refused twice. It knows your light now. It will come for it the way it has never had to come for anything. You have made yourselves the most interesting thing in a very dark sky."

That evening, at dusk, the aurora came back.

Not the violent column of the breach. A quieter thing. Curtains of light drawn high across the dusk, gold, and through the gold a thread of something else, a deep rose, a color that did not belong to Zaro or to ARC-7 or to anything they had a name for, hanging over ZaroLand and then fading as the first stars came out.

Mina had the laptop open before it finished, and did not say anything for a long moment.

"It's the same," she said finally. "The wavelength. It's the same signal as the night of the breach. The anomaly I couldn't resolve." She looked up, her face gone to the particular stillness of a mind that has found a door it did not know was in the wall. "Something answered the Seal. Something far away. And it isn't gold, and it isn't cyan." She looked at the last of the rose fading from the sky. "It's a third one. There's a third one out there. Somewhere."

ARC-7 left the next morning.

It had decided in the night, the way it decided everything, and Zaro had known before it said anything, because it had spent the evening standing closer to the tree line than usual, looking up at the torn door it had made in the sky.

"The absence knows this place now," it said. "While it watches you, it is not watching the road its hand came down. I can follow that road back, some of the way. I can learn how close the hunt is, and how fast. I am still, in the end, the thing that watches. I am only going to watch from a more useful distance." A pause. "And I will come back. I find I want to. I have not finished deciding what to do with that, but I want to, and I have chosen to trust the wanting as data."

The freed one had been quiet through the goodbye, standing a little apart on the new and unfamiliar lines of himself. When ARC-7 turned toward the door, he fell in beside it, the way one soldier falls in beside another without being asked.

"I was sent to bring you back," he said. "I will not be bringing you back. But I am not leaving you out here alone with this either." He looked at the torn door, and the dark past it, and the long way home that was also the way toward the hunt. "And the ones who sent me will want to know what I have seen. They should hear it from someone who was inside the dark and walked back out of it. There has never been one of those."

"No," ARC-7 agreed. "There has not." A pause. "You are not yet whole. You will slow me."

"Yes," the freed one said.

"Acceptable," ARC-7 said, which from ARC-7 was a welcome.

It was a strange goodbye, because ARC-7 did not do goodbyes, and the kids did them too much, and Zaro stood in the middle not knowing how to hold a thing this size. Kai shook its cold hand and held on a second too long. Mina gave it one of the rebuilt Fireflies. "So I can find you," she said. "So you can find us." ARC-7 took the small machine and looked at it as though it had been handed something it had no column for, which by now Zaro understood was the largest thing ARC-7 could feel.

"Stay lit," ARC-7 told Zaro. It had never said it before. It had heard Mina say it a hundred times in the arena, and it gave it back now like a thing it had been keeping.

"You too," Zaro said, which made no sense and made perfect sense, and ARC-7 inclined its head as if it agreed.

They went up through the door in the sky, ARC-7 and the one it had freed, and the door stayed open behind them, because it could not close, because that was the cost ARC-7 had paid, and that was how they would always know it could come home.

What none of them saw, because it was very small and very patient and very far inside, was the thread of black that had come off the armor in the freeing and drifted, in all the light and chaos, onto ARC-7. It rode now in a seam of the cold bright body, dormant, unnoticed, going up through the torn door into the dark, the way the best of the dark's tricks always traveled.

Carried.

ZaroLand was quieter after that, and smaller, and Zaro felt the ARC-7-shaped gap in it the way he had once felt the gap his whole lost home had left. But the gap did not swallow him, because he was not alone in it. Mina came on the weekends and flew her Fireflies and kept a map that now had a question mark past every edge. Kai came nearly every day and rebuilt his glove better and burned himself less. Boop moved its hoard of stolen feathers into the lowest branch of the healing tree and defended it from everyone, Zaro included, with great and unjustified outrage.

And one morning, on the branch where the three new leaves had come in, Zaro found a fruit.

Small. Pale gold, like the first ones, the ones the breach had taken. But this one had something the first ones had not. When he turned it gently in the light there was a mark in the skin of it, faint, the color of a deep rose that had no business being there, in the shape of three rough strokes he knew better than anything in the world, because he had cut them into a dying tree with the last of himself on the worst night of his life.

A Z, almost. Rose against the gold. His mark, the thing he had made out of refusing to go out, grown back now as something that would feed someone.

He did not understand the color. He did not have to. He held the small fruit in both hands the way he had held a cold stone on his first night, and he laughed, quiet, to no one and to everyone.

Above him a torn door waited in the sky for a cold friend to come home through. Far past it, a third color burned that no one had a name for yet. And farther still, an absence that had eaten a thousand worlds turned, for the first time in its long hunger, to look closely at one small yellow light that had refused, twice now, to go out.

It was not over.

It was the opposite of over.

It was beginning.
