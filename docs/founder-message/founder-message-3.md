# Founder Message #3 — The Decision

*December 11–12, 2025*

Around 7 PM Dubai time on December 11, 2025, I get a notification that someone deposited funds into one of my dormant wallets. I know what's about to happen.

I look at the chart. The chart is burning. I see the real-time drain of the wallets, one after another.

My kids are around me. They're looking at their father in devastation.

The amount, in absolute terms, is about seventy thousand dollars. It doesn't kill me. It doesn't end the project. But the feeling of being violated — of watching everything I'd been building for six months break in six minutes — is one of the biggest things I've ever lived through.

I open my phone. I have ten treasury wallets, not one. Months earlier I'd split the treasury supply across multiple wallets specifically so a single compromise couldn't take everything at once. Each wallet holds only ZARO — no ETH, no BNB, no gas. The hacker has to deposit ETH into each wallet before he can move the ZARO out of it. That's the road block. That's what's slowing him down. That's the only thing buying me time.

So I do the only thing I can do. I create a temporary wallet. I watch for the hacker to dump ZARO into the Uniswap pool — the pool is safe, the supply that goes through the pool is buyable by anyone — and the moment he sells, ETH comes out the other side and I race him for it.

I'm switching between ten wallets on my phone, trying to spot a glimpse of ETH appearing in a wallet that had only ZARO a second ago. When I see it, I know that wallet has just been gas-funded by the hacker. The next move from that wallet is going to be a ZARO dump. I have seconds.

I have to steal my own money from my own wallet and race him to whatever ETH he just generated.

The big chunk was lost before I caught up. Then the BSC and Solana bridged assets drained too. He took ETH. He took BNB. He took USDT. If anyone traces the hashes and wallets later, the fight has a timeline. My temp-wallet purchases are interleaved with the drain transactions on Etherscan. It's all there.

A friend comes over. He helps me think. He helps me regroup. I'm shivering.

The structural defenses I'd built took six months to construct and they did what they were designed to do. The renounced contract held. The 255-year LP lock held. The ten-wallet treasury structure slowed the attack by orders of magnitude. The bridge backing on Ethereum was never at risk. Everything that was engineered to be unbreakable was, in fact, unbreakable.

What I didn't anticipate was a Trojan horse. The breach didn't come through the wallet infrastructure. It came through a piece of malware delivered as a beautiful website pretending to be an AI tool. The vector was a door I'd opened myself, on my own machine, because the wrapping looked legitimate.

---

Around 6 AM Dubai time on December 12, after no sleep, I write a message and send it to everyone I know. I also post a version of it on X. The message goes out before any of the criminal reports are filed, before the forensic firm is engaged, before the buyback begins. It's the first move of the response, written in the wreckage:

Some people filled the form and were bought back at the price they had paid — even when their entry price was twenty times the price ZARO was trading at the morning after the crash. I did not blame anyone for taking that exit. I had offered it.

Some saw the recovery and held.

Some bought more during the recovery and are sitting at meaningful profit now.

The buyback offer was honored to every participant who registered. Then I closed the form, slept for a few hours, and woke up to start filing the criminal reports. The structural details of what came next — the Dubai Police case, the forensic engagement, the Public Security Advisory, the open-market buyback over the weeks that followed, the hardware wallet custody — are documented on the [/history page](https://www.zarocoin.xyz/history#response) on zarocoin.xyz.

Before December 11, ZaroVerse existed in broad strokes. I had a universe — a cosmic saga, a CMYK power system, a yellow protagonist, characters that would eventually populate three books. But it was high-level. Outlines, sketches, intentions. I'd been running it as a side creative project on top of an established career, monitoring the build from a distance, letting the heart of it move at its own pace while the operational discipline of my other work stayed in its own lane.

The night of December 11 changed that.

Zaro is the part of me that wanted to build something timeless — clean, generational, story-first, not designed for quick returns and not built to be a financial instrument. The heart of the project.

ARC-7 is the part of me that runs a regulated global fintech business — disciplined, systems-aware, monitoring from a distance, deliberately keeping its hands off the heart's work. The brain of the operator. The cyan to Zaro's yellow.

Mina and Kai are the friends and family who came over that night, and the ones I called the next morning, and the ones who carried me through the days that followed. They became the people in the story because they were the people in the room.

What happened on December 11 was the moment ARC-7 had to break his own rules. The brain had to stop watching from the dome and come down into the burning. Announce himself. Take this ultra-seriously. Bring the same operational discipline to the heart's project that had built everything else.

ZaroVerse stopped being broad strokes that night. It became specific because it had to.

The Trojan crystal that hides the Ink inside a beautiful object — the central threat mechanism of the universe — was written into the canon in the weeks after December 11, because that was the threat I had just survived. The burned tree, the scorched dome, the moment when the protagonist looks at everything he'd been building and watches it nearly end — those scenes weren't planned. They were lived first, then written.

*The Yellow Spark*, Book 1 of the ZaroVerse saga, is the autobiography of the start of ZaroVerse. I was just inside of it when it began.
